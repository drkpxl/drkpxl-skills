# Copy Frameworks Reference

Deep reference for the **copywriting** skill: headline formulas, persuasion frameworks,
page templates, proof and objection patterns, and natural transition phrases. Pull from
this when writing or restructuring page copy; the SKILL.md covers the core principles and
workflow.

---

## Headline Formulas

Pick the formula that matches what the page most needs to prove. Specific beats generic
every time — fill the brackets with real outcomes, numbers, and customer language.

### Outcome-driven
- "{Achieve outcome} without {pain point}" — *Ship faster without breaking production*
- "{Achieve outcome} in {timeframe}" — *Launch your store in an afternoon*
- "Get {desirable result}, even if {common obstacle}"
- "From {bad state} to {good state} in {timeframe}"

### Category / positioning
- "The {category} for {audience}" — *The CRM for solo consultants*
- "{Product} is the {familiar thing}, but for {twist}"
- "The fastest way to {job to be done}"
- "{Category}, reimagined for {audience}"

### Problem-forward
- "Never {unpleasant event} again" — *Never lose a draft again*
- "Stop {painful behavior}. Start {better behavior}."
- "Tired of {frustration}?"
- "{Question highlighting main pain point}" — *Still chasing invoices by email?*

### Proof / specificity
- "{Number} {audience} use {product} to {outcome}"
- "Join {number} {audience} who {achieved outcome}"
- "The {outcome} {authority} trust"

### Curiosity / mechanism
- "How {audience} {achieve outcome} (without {expected cost})"
- "The {adjective} way to {outcome}"
- "What if {desirable scenario}?"

**Rule of thumb:** the headline makes a promise; the subheadline makes it believable.

## Subheadline Patterns

The subheadline does one of these jobs — choose deliberately:
- **Clarify** what the product literally is, if the headline was aspirational.
- **Quantify** the promise with a number, timeframe, or proof.
- **Qualify** the audience so the right person feels addressed.
- **Address the objection** the headline raises ("…and no, you don't need a developer").

Keep it to 1-2 sentences. If the headline is concrete, the subheadline can add emotion; if
the headline is emotional, the subheadline should add concreteness.

## Persuasion Frameworks

Use these to sequence an argument down a page or within a section. They are scaffolding,
not scripts — collapse or expand stages to fit the page.

### PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solution
1. **Problem** — name the pain in the reader's words.
2. **Agitate** — make the cost of the problem vivid (time, money, stress, risk).
3. **Solution** — present the product as the resolution.
Best for: landing pages, pain-aware audiences.

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
1. **Attention** — headline that stops the scroll.
2. **Interest** — relevant benefit or intriguing detail.
3. **Desire** — proof, outcomes, transformation.
4. **Action** — clear CTA.
Best for: full pages, cold traffic.

### BAB — Before, After, Bridge
1. **Before** — the reader's current frustrating reality.
2. **After** — life once the problem is solved.
3. **Bridge** — the product as the path between them.
Best for: hero sections, short landing pages, emotional positioning.

### FAB — Feature, Advantage, Benefit
- **Feature** — what it is.
- **Advantage** — what it does.
- **Benefit** — what that means for the customer.
Best for: feature pages and benefit bullets. Always end on the benefit.

### The 4 U's (for headlines/CTAs)
Strong headlines tend to be **Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific**. Hit at least two.

### PASTOR (long-form sales pages)
Problem → Amplify → Story/Solution → Transformation/Testimony → Offer → Response.
Best for: long sales pages where trust must be built before the ask.

## Page Templates (section sequences)

Suggested section order. Adapt to the product; not every page needs every section.

### Homepage
1. Hero: headline + subheadline + primary CTA (+ supporting visual)
2. Social proof strip (logos, user count, rating)
3. Core benefits (3-5, benefit-led)
4. How it works (3-4 steps)
5. Deeper feature/benefit sections (one idea each)
6. Testimonials / case study
7. Objection handling (FAQ, comparison)
8. Final CTA with risk reversal

### Landing Page (single offer, matched to traffic)
1. Hero matched to the ad/source promise
2. Problem/agitation (PAS)
3. Solution + key benefits
4. Proof (testimonials, numbers, guarantees)
5. How it works
6. FAQ / objection handling
7. Single repeated CTA
*One message, one CTA. Remove site nav that leaks attention.*

### Pricing Page
1. Brief value reminder headline
2. Plan comparison table (recommended plan visually marked)
3. "Which plan is right for me?" guidance
4. Feature/plan FAQ
5. Risk reversal (free trial, money-back, no card required)
6. CTA per plan

### Feature Page
1. Feature headline tied to the outcome it enables
2. Feature → advantage → benefit (FAB)
3. Use cases / examples / screenshots
4. Proof relevant to this feature
5. Related features or integrations
6. CTA to try or buy

### About Page
1. Why we exist (mission / origin)
2. The problem we set out to solve
3. How that connects to the customer's benefit
4. Team / values / credibility
5. CTA (yes, About pages convert too)

## Social Proof Formats

Match the proof to the doubt it answers:
- **Logos** — "real companies trust this" (answers legitimacy).
- **Stat / count** — "X customers," "Y processed" (answers popularity/scale).
- **Testimonial** — specific, named, outcome-focused (answers "will it work for someone like me?").
- **Case study** — before/after with numbers (answers "what results can I expect?").
- **Ratings / reviews** — star count + source (answers third-party validation).
- **Trust badges** — security, compliance, guarantees (answers risk).

Best testimonials lead with a result, name a specific obstacle overcome, and are attributed
to a real, relatable person. Avoid vague praise ("Great product!").

## Objection Handling Patterns

Surface the hesitation, then dissolve it:
- **FAQ** — answer the real top-of-mind objections, not softballs.
- **Comparison table** — vs. alternatives or vs. the status quo / doing nothing.
- **Guarantee / risk reversal** — money-back, free trial, no credit card, cancel anytime.
- **"You might be thinking…"** — name the objection in the reader's voice, then answer it.
- **Proof adjacency** — place a testimonial that addresses an objection right next to it.

Common objections to pre-empt: price, time/effort to switch, "will it work for my case,"
trust/security, lock-in, and learning curve.

## Natural Transition Phrases

Use these to connect sections so the page reads as one argument rather than disconnected
blocks. Use sparingly — the best transition is often a logical sequence of ideas, not a
connective phrase. Avoid starting every section with the same word.

### Introducing a benefit or result
- "So you can…"
- "Which means…"
- "The result:"
- "That's how you…"

### Adding evidence
- "Here's the proof:"
- "Don't just take our word for it."
- "The numbers back it up."

### Handling a turn / objection
- "But here's the thing…"
- "You might be wondering…"
- "Worried about {X}? Don't be."

### Moving to the next idea
- "Once that's done…"
- "From there…"
- "Now for the part most people miss."

### Driving to the CTA
- "Ready to {outcome}?"
- "Here's how to start."
- "Everything you need to {outcome} — in one place."

**Caution:** these are seasoning. If copy leans on transition phrases to feel cohesive, the
underlying argument probably isn't ordered well. Fix the order first.
